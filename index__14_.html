HOW TO PLAY

You'll hear spatial premises like "B is North of C." Your job: was the n-back-ago premise the same kind as this one? Press MATCH or NO_MATCH.

At N=1, compare to the previous trial. At N=2, two trials back, and so on, up to N=10 for expert-level training. Typical adult working-memory span is around 4 items — anything past N=5 is exceptional.

The Da Vinci Method

Da Vinci's notebooks pulled the swirl of water around an obstacle into the swirl of blood around heart valves and into the curl of hair in a portrait. Three universes apart, one structural insight. This is the cognitive move the directions in IMAGI-WORLD are meant to train.

Directions are not fixed labels. Do not memorize "North = generalize" or "West = opposite." That turns the game into lookup and kills relational reasoning. A direction is a positional marker whose meaning is constructed fresh from (a) the two letters you anchor, (b) the cognitive frame you build around the pair, and (c) the contrast the other directions in the trial set up by opposition.

Anchor across radical domain gaps. If B and C are letters, don't decode them both as biology. Let B be a beehive and C be a courtroom. Let L be lightning and M be mythology. The wider the cross-domain leap, the more relational work the direction operator has to do — and that work is the training target.

Each trial, build a new operator. The "same" North can mean unwitting structural inheritance in one trial, biological pattern lent to an abstract system in the next, and engineering-tension-mirrored-as-acoustic-tension in the next. The adaptivity itself is the skill.

The procedure — do this every trial

The premise is given to you (the letters and the directions are fixed by the game). What you build is the meaning. Every trial, in order:

Step 1 — Anchor. Give each letter a concrete thing from a domain as far from the others as you can reach (B → a beehive, C → a courtroom). Never leave a letter as a bare symbol.

Step 2 — Read the direction as a relation between those anchors. The direction word is a prompt to find a relationship, not a label to look up. "B is North of C" asks: what relationship runs from the courtroom to the beehive? You invent it on the spot (e.g. the beehive's structure silently shapes the courtroom's). That invented relationship is your "operator" for this trial.

Step 3 — Hold the shape, drop the surface. Keep the relationship (the operator), not the beehive or the courtroom. The thing you carry across trials is the shape, never the domains.

Step 4 — When the n-back trial comes, compare shapes. MATCH if the relationship you build now is the same shape as the one N trials back; NO_MATCH if it is a different shape. The letters and directions will look different — only the shape decides.

One thing changes between the simple modes and the transform modes. In Modes 1–3 a direction is a relationship you freely invent between anchors. In Modes 4–6 a direction is a type of change the premise already implies — a flip into the opposite (North↔South or East↔West) is an inversion; a ninety-degree turn (e.g. North→East) is a rotation. Your Da Vinci job there is to anchor a domain that feels that exact change, then match on the type of change. Same method, the difference is only what the direction is asking you to build.

Worked Mode 1 examples — "B is North of C"

Three different anchorings of the same premise, each producing a different "North" operator:

1. B = beehive, C = courtroom. "North" = structural inheritance across eras: hexagonal packing silently shapes how jury seating packs people.

2. B = breath, C = currency. "North" = bodily prototype lent to an abstract system: breath's rhythm is what money's flow unknowingly mimics.

3. B = bridge cables, C = cathedral chant. "North" = physical tension mirrored as acoustic tension: distributed cable load is the shape of voices in harmonic suspension.

Two more — "L is West of M"

4. L = a tax, M = gravity. "West" = an unseen constant pull that bends every trajectory toward a centre: income curves toward the state as motion curves toward mass.

5. L = lichen, M = manuscript marginalia. "West" = encroachment on unused edges: both colonize the margins a structure ignored and slowly extend its meaning.

"North" meant three different things across 1–3; the direction is the prompt, the operator is what you construct. The match is on the direction itself: two North trials MATCH; North vs West is NO_MATCH.

This adaptive anchoring engages frontal-polar cortex and turns direction-matching into the kind of cross-domain relational reasoning that transfers to analogy and matrix-reasoning tests.

Worked Mode 2 examples — "A is North of B, and West of C" (Semantic Tensor)

One node A is pinned to two anchors on perpendicular axes — one of N/S, one of E/W. Anchor each across a domain gap and build the two operators, but the scored class is the pinning pair (e.g. North-and-East). Two trials MATCH iff the pair recurs.

6. "A N of B, and E of C." A = tuning fork, B = verdict, C = estuary: resonance settling one, driving the other. Pinning pair = N&E; another N&E trial → MATCH.

7. NO_MATCH. "A N of B, and W of C." A = keystone, B = rumour, C = vaccine. Pinning pair = N&W. Since N&W ≠ N&E, this is NO_MATCH against example 6.

Worked Mode 3 examples — "A is North and East of B, as C is North and East of D" (Homology)

The premise gives a compound corner direction — North-and-East, North-and-West, South-and-East, or South-and-West — holding across both pairs. Read that corner as one constructed 2D operator binding A↔B, anchor across a domain gap, and verify it lands on C↔D. Two trials MATCH iff they share the same corner.

8. MATCH. "A NE of B, as C NE of D." A = glacier, B = language; C = interest, D = trauma. NE is your 2D operator across both pairs; another NE trial matches.

9. NO_MATCH. An NE trial, then an SW trial (X = comet, Y = verdict). Both valid, but NE ≠ SW — a different corner operator. You match the corner class, not the domains.

Worked Mode 4 examples — "North to South of F, as East to West of J" (Algebraic Symmetry)

Each clause describes one letter undergoing one change. You classify each change into the four-class vocabulary below, which gives the trial a pair of classes; the MATCH judgment is whether a later trial carries that same pair. Anchor each letter so you can feel the change, but score on the class.

The whole answer space is just four kinds of change (this is true for Modes 4, 5 and 6). Every change a clause can describe is one of: vertical inversion (North↔South — a flip on the up/down axis), horizontal inversion (East↔West — a flip on the side/side axis), clockwise rotation (a 90° turn the clockwise way: N→E, E→S, S→W, W→N), or counter-clockwise rotation (a 90° turn the other way: N→W, W→S, S→E, E→N). The two inversions are flips; the two rotations are quarter-turns. A flip is never the same class as a turn, and the two turns are different classes from each other.

10. "N→S of F, as E→W of J." F = a star: fusion flipped to collapse = vertical inversion. J = an oath: truth crossed to perjury = horizontal inversion. Pair: {vertical, horizontal}.

11. NO_MATCH. "N→E of R, as N→E of T." R = a seed: dormancy into sprout; T = a tide: slack into surge — both clockwise rotations. A rotation pair never matches an inversion pair.

Worked Mode 5 examples — "D1 to D2 of X, D2 to D3 of Y, so D1 to D3 of X" (Composition)

Unlike Mode 6, Mode 5 states the composite in the third clause — your job is to anchor X and Y as two different domains, build each step as its own constructed operator, fuse them at the shared midpoint, and verify the stated composite is the move they truly add up to. Mode 5 composites resolve to the same four classes as Modes 4 and 6; these four span them, then show a NO_MATCH.

12. Vertical inversion. "N→W of X, W→S of Y, so N→S of X." X = glacier: advance into lateral grinding. Y = cross-examination: pressure boring down till the witness caves. Fused N→S.

13. Horizontal inversion (NO_MATCH w/12). "E→S of X, S→W of Y, so E→W of X." X = coral reef: growth into shadow-death. Y = propaganda: silence into counter-signal. Fused E→W — different class.

14. Clockwise rotation. "N→S of X, S→E of Y, so N→E of X." X = supernova: peak into collapse. Y = a sentence: silence into syntax. An inversion step can compose to a rotation.

15. Counter-clockwise rotation. "N→S of X, S→W of Y, so N→W of X." X = printing press: text-flood into censorship. Y = tectonic fault: pressure released on a new line. Opposite-handed to 14, NO_MATCH.

16. Across-trial MATCH. "N→W of X, W→S of Y, so N→S of X." X = coastline: wild shore into promenade. Y = bureaucracy: plan into forgotten rules. Fused N→S — same class as 12, MATCH.

Worked Mode 6 examples — "D1 to D2 of X, D2 to D3 of Y, so invention of Z" (Recursive Invention)

Same chain as Mode 5, but the composite is never named — you must build it, bind it to Z, and carry it forward. Mode 6 chains resolve to the same four classes as Modes 4 and 5 (vertical inversion, horizontal inversion, clockwise rotation, counter-clockwise rotation); these five span all four, then show a NO_MATCH and the recall move.

17. Vertical inversion. "N→E of X, E→S of Y, so invention of Z." X = vow: devotion to law. Y resumes at E: law to its tomb. Fuse → N→S. Z = devotion fossilized.

18. Horizontal inversion. "E→N of X, N→W of Y, so invention of Z." X = exile: home to estrangement. Y resumes at N: into its opposite. Fuse → E→W. NO_MATCH w/17.

19. Clockwise rotation. "N→S of X, S→E of Y, so invention of Z." X = avalanche: peak to ruin. Y resumes at S: ruin to commerce. Fuse → N→E. Z = a fall become exchange.

20. Counter-clockwise rotation. "N→S of X, S→W of Y, so invention of Z." X = melody: sound to silence. Y resumes at S: silence to a carved valley. Fuse → N→W. NO_MATCH w/19.

21. Recall move (MATCH). "N→E of Z, E→S of K, so invention of P." Z isn't fresh — recall ex17's Z (devotion fossilized), fuse it through K, forge P. Composite N→S — matches 17.

Across all of these, notice the constant: the letters and directions are never decoded the same way twice. You build the operator fresh from the domains you anchor, and what you match across trials is the shape you built — never the surface. That construction — not any rule you could memorize — is the entire skill.

Now your turn — three practice rounds

Round 1 (Mode 1) — "G is North of P." Run the four steps before reading the model. Step 1: anchor G and P in two far-apart domains. Step 2: what relationship runs from P to G? Step 3: name the shape. Step 4: imagine the next trial is "T is North of K" — would your shape have to recur for it to be a MATCH?

Model (yours can differ): G = gut microbiome, P = parliament. "North" = an invisible majority quietly governing the visible body it inhabits. Build that same hidden-steers-host shape next trial → MATCH; otherwise NO_MATCH.

Round 2 (Mode 4, a transform mode) — "North to South of D, as East to West of M." In Mode 4 you classify each of the trial's two changes into the four-class vocabulary, giving the trial a pair of classes. The MATCH judgment is whether a later trial carries the same pair of classes. First classify both changes here, then anchor a domain that feels each, then say what a matching later trial would need.

Model: "North to South" = vertical inversion (tyrant into martyr). "East to West" = horizontal inversion (defector to mirror enemy). Pair: {vertical, horizontal}. Later trial MATCHES only with that same pair; rotations are NO_MATCH.

Round 3 (Mode 6, the chain) — "North to West of X, West to South of Y, so invention of Z." Note the structure: X carries the first step (North→West), Y carries the second step (West→South), and they meet at the shared midpoint West — Y picks up exactly where X left off. Anchor X and Y as two different things, build each one's step, then fuse the two steps — joined at that West pivot — into one whole move from North all the way to South. Classify that composite, and bind it to Z. Then say what you'd recall if Z returns two trials later.

Model: X = wildfire: destruction into cleared ground. Y = bureaucracy: ground into self-perpetuating rules. Fused North→South = vertical inversion. Z = wild force become the order that forgets it; recall Z if it returns.

Tips

The letters (B, C, D, etc.) are doing three jobs at once, not one. First, they're your anchor points for the Da Vinci method — where you bind a domain and build the cross-domain operator. Second, they're your recall handles — when a letter reappears in a later trial, the letter is the cue that pulls back the relational frame you constructed for it the first time. Third, they're transformation targets — when interference patterns deliberately re-present a prior letter in a new structural role (subject becomes object, anchor becomes pivot), you have to hold the old binding alongside the new one and decide whether the structure has been preserved, inverted, rotated, or replaced. The letters are not noise and they are not just placeholders for the directional pattern — they are the axis along which your prior relational construction gets re-evaluated.

Use Adaptive N to let the game auto-tune difficulty (the Jaeggi protocol).

Cognitive Interference creates logical conflict between consecutive trials at TWO layers: (1) letter-level — letters from the previous trial reappear in conflicting roles (chain, role inversion, intervening-item, mode-specific chains); (2) direction-level — directions are biased toward opposite ("S is North of J" → "J is South of S"), perpendicular ("N → E"), or identical-with-letter-chain (transitive setups). Higher = harder, less drift. The default is 60% — a balanced level that produces meaningful interference without overwhelming new users. Push toward 75–90% for expert training.

d' (d-prime) measures discrimination ability from signal detection theory. Higher is better. 1.0 = decent, 2.0 = excellent.
